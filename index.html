Renewable Energy Impacts on the Transportation Industry of India

Opportunities, Implications, and the Road to a Sustainable Mobility Ecosystem

February 2026
Read the Research ↓
Section 01

Introduction

The transportation sector stands at a critical juncture in India's developmental narrative. As the world's third-largest emitter of greenhouse gases, India faces the dual challenge of sustaining rapid economic growth while mitigating the severe environmental consequences of fossil fuel dependency.

Historically, India's transport infrastructure has been heavily reliant on imported crude oil, which not only strains the national exchequer but also contributes significantly to urban air pollution and climate change. With the automotive industry contributing to approximately 7% of India's GDP, any structural shift in this sector has profound macroeconomic implications. The transition to renewable energy-driven transport is not merely a technological upgrade but a systemic overhaul necessitated by the urgent need for energy security and environmental stewardship [1].

This research examines how renewable energy technologies — including electric vehicles (EVs), green hydrogen fuel cells, biofuels, and electrified rail systems — are beginning to reshape India's transportation landscape. The analysis draws upon data from the International Energy Agency (IEA), NITI Aayog, the Ministry of New and Renewable Energy (MNRE), and the Council on Energy, Environment and Water (CEEW) to provide a comprehensive assessment of both the opportunities and the complex challenges that lie ahead. The study also incorporates a chemical engineering perspective to explain the fundamental science behind these technologies, making it relevant to both policy audiences and technical communities [2].

Section 02

Problem Statement: India's Transport Crisis

The scale of India's transport problem is staggering. As of 2024, over 350 million vehicles are registered in the country, a number that has been growing at a compound annual rate of nearly 10% over the past decade. This explosive growth in motorization has been driven by rising disposable incomes, rapid urbanization — with over 600 million Indians now living in urban areas — and the absence, until recently, of viable public transport alternatives in most Indian cities. The consequences of this unchecked growth are felt across environmental, economic, and public health dimensions [3].

Environmental and Health Impact

The environmental cost of India's fossil-fuel-dependent transport is immense. The transport sector is responsible for approximately 14% of the country's total CO₂ emissions, consuming nearly 70% of all diesel produced domestically and 99.6% of all petrol. But the damage goes far beyond carbon dioxide. Vehicles emit a complex cocktail of pollutants including particulate matter (PM2.5 and PM10), nitrogen oxides (NOₓ), carbon monoxide (CO), sulfur dioxide (SO₂), and volatile organic compounds (VOCs). According to the World Air Quality Report 2023 published by IQAir, 21 of the world's 30 most polluted cities are in India, with vehicular emissions identified as a primary contributor in every case [4].

The public health implications are devastating. A landmark study published in The Lancet estimated that air pollution — of which vehicular emissions are a major component — contributed to approximately 1.67 million premature deaths in India in 2019. Respiratory diseases, cardiovascular complications, and lung cancer rates in Indian urban centres are significantly elevated compared to rural areas, with medical researchers drawing a direct correlation to exposure to vehicular pollutants. The economic cost of this health burden is estimated at 1.36% of GDP, according to the World Bank [5].

Economic Vulnerability and Energy Security

Beyond the environmental dimension, India's transport sector represents a critical vulnerability in national energy security. The country imports over 85% of its crude oil requirements, making it the world's third-largest oil importer after China and the United States. In the fiscal year 2023-24, India's petroleum import bill exceeded $150 billion, representing roughly 25% of total merchandise imports and placing enormous pressure on the current account balance. This dependency leaves the Indian economy acutely sensitive to geopolitical disruptions — as demonstrated during the 2022 Russia-Ukraine conflict when crude oil prices spiked above $120 per barrel, triggering domestic fuel price hikes and inflationary pressures across the supply chain [6].

The strategic imperative for decoupling transportation from imported petroleum is therefore not just an environmental argument but a fundamental question of economic resilience and national sovereignty. Renewable energy technologies offer a credible pathway to achieve this decoupling, and the following sections examine the most promising avenues.

Section 03

Renewable Energy Technologies in Transportation

The transition from fossil fuels to renewable energy in India's transport sector is being driven by four principal technologies, each at a different stage of maturity and adoption. Together, they represent a comprehensive strategy that addresses different segments of the mobility ecosystem — from urban last-mile connectivity to long-haul freight logistics.

3.1 Electric Vehicles (EVs)

Electric mobility is the most commercially mature and rapidly scaling clean transport solution available today. An electric vehicle replaces the conventional internal combustion engine (ICE) with an electric motor powered by a rechargeable lithium-ion battery pack. The efficiency advantage is dramatic: a typical electric motor converts 85-90% of electrical energy into mechanical energy at the wheels, compared to just 20-30% for a petrol engine, where the majority of energy is lost as waste heat. This translates into significantly lower per-kilometer operating costs — typically ₹1-1.5/km for an EV versus ₹5-7/km for a comparable petrol vehicle [7].

In India, the EV revolution has been led by the two-wheeler and three-wheeler segments, which together account for over 90% of EV sales. Companies such as Ola Electric, Ather Energy, TVS Motor, and Bajaj Auto have launched increasingly competitive electric scooter models, while Tata Motors dominates the passenger car EV segment with the Nexon EV and Tiago EV. The Indian EV market recorded 1.52 million unit sales in 2024, a 49% year-on-year increase, signaling that the inflection point for mass adoption may have arrived. However, the "greenness" of an EV is ultimately determined by the source of electricity used for charging. With India's grid still deriving approximately 55% of its power from coal, the lifecycle carbon footprint of an EV in India is lower than a petrol vehicle but not zero. The government's target of 500 GW of renewable energy capacity by 2030 is therefore critical to maximizing the climate benefits of EV adoption [8].

3.2 Biofuels and Ethanol Blending

Biofuels represent an important transitional solution, particularly for the existing fleet of hundreds of millions of ICE vehicles that cannot be retired overnight. The Government of India has pursued an aggressive Ethanol Blending Programme (EBP), which has successfully increased the proportion of ethanol in petrol from less than 2% in 2014 to over 12% by 2024, with a target of 20% (E20) by 2025-26. Ethanol, produced primarily from sugarcane molasses, surplus rice, and damaged foodgrains through the process of fermentation, is a partially oxygenated fuel that burns more completely than pure petrol, reducing emissions of carbon monoxide and unburned hydrocarbons [9].

The programme has the dual benefit of reducing crude oil imports — the government estimates annual savings of ₹30,000 crore at E20 levels — and providing supplementary income to farmers through the procurement of feedstock. However, the programme is not without controversy. Critics point to the water-intensive nature of sugarcane cultivation, concerns about diverting food crops to fuel production, and the fact that ethanol blending reduces (but does not eliminate) tailpipe emissions. Nevertheless, as a practical, near-term measure to reduce India's oil dependency, the EBP has been one of the most successful policy interventions in the energy sector.

3.3 Green Hydrogen Fuel Cells

For the segments of the transport industry where battery-electric solutions face fundamental limitations — primarily long-haul trucking, intercity buses, and potentially maritime and aviation applications — green hydrogen produced through the electrolysis of water using renewable electricity offers a transformative alternative. A hydrogen fuel cell generates electricity on board the vehicle through an electrochemical reaction between hydrogen and oxygen, with pure water vapor as the only emission. The refueling time is comparable to conventional diesel — approximately 10-15 minutes for a full tank — and the range can exceed 500 km for heavy-duty vehicles, addressing the two key limitations of battery-electric technology for these applications [10].

India's National Green Hydrogen Mission, launched in January 2023 with an initial outlay of ₹19,744 crore, aims to make the country a global hub for green hydrogen production and utilization. The mission targets production capacity of 5 million metric tonnes per annum (MTPA) by 2030. Pilot projects have already been initiated for hydrogen-powered buses in Delhi and Leh, and Indian Oil Corporation is developing hydrogen dispensing infrastructure at select retail outlets. While the technology is currently expensive — green hydrogen costs approximately $4-5/kg compared to $1.5-2/kg for grey hydrogen produced from natural gas — costs are projected to fall below $2/kg by 2030 as electrolyzer technology matures and renewable electricity costs continue to decline.

3.4 Railway Electrification

Indian Railways, the fourth-largest railway network in the world, has undertaken one of the most ambitious electrification programmes globally. As of December 2024, approximately 93% of the broad-gauge network — spanning over 59,000 route kilometers — has been electrified, up from just 40% in 2014. The shift from diesel to electric traction reduces direct emissions to zero at the point of use, while also being significantly more energy-efficient. An electric locomotive is approximately three times more energy-efficient than a diesel locomotive in terms of net traction energy per gross tonne-kilometer. Indian Railways has committed to achieving "net-zero carbon emissions" by 2030, and is supplementing grid-based electrification with dedicated solar and wind installations along railway corridors [11].

Section 04

Chemical Engineering Perspective

A rigorous understanding of the chemical reactions and electrochemical processes underpinning transport energy systems is essential for evaluating their efficiency, environmental impact, and scalability. This section examines the key reactions from a chemical engineering standpoint.

4.1 Combustion of Petrol (Octane)

The internal combustion engine operates on the principle of controlled explosive combustion of hydrocarbon fuels. When octane (C₈H₁₈), a primary component of petrol, is ignited in the presence of atmospheric oxygen within the engine cylinder, it undergoes a rapid exothermic oxidation reaction producing carbon dioxide, water vapor, and heat energy that drives the piston.

Balanced Combustion Reaction (Petrol/Octane)

2C₈H₁₈ + 25O₂ → 16CO₂ + 18H₂O + Energy (Heat)

Each litre of petrol burned produces approximately 2.31 kg of CO₂. At India's average vehicle mileage of 15 km/litre, this equates to roughly 154 g of CO₂ per kilometer traveled.

4.2 Ethanol Fermentation

Bioethanol production relies on the anaerobic fermentation of simple sugars (glucose) by yeast organisms (Saccharomyces cerevisiae). This biological process, fundamental to India's Ethanol Blending Programme, converts agricultural feedstock into a liquid fuel that can be blended with petrol or used independently.

Ethanol Fermentation Reaction

C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂

While CO₂ is released during fermentation, it is approximately equal to the CO₂ absorbed by the crop during its growth phase through photosynthesis, making it a near-carbon-neutral cycle when assessed on a lifecycle basis.

4.3 Hydrogen Fuel Cell

The Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) fuel cell is the most widely used type for transport applications. At the anode, hydrogen gas is oxidized, releasing protons (H⁺) and electrons. The electrons flow through an external circuit generating electricity, while the protons pass through the membrane to combine with oxygen at the cathode, forming water.

Hydrogen Fuel Cell Electrochemical Reaction

2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O + Electrical Energy

The only byproduct is pure water. Fuel cell efficiency (50-60%) significantly exceeds that of internal combustion engines (20-30%), and when powered by green hydrogen, this represents a truly zero-emission transport pathway.

4.4 Lithium-Ion Battery: Electrochemistry

The lithium-ion battery, the core energy storage device in all modern EVs, operates on the principle of reversible intercalation. During discharge, lithium ions (Li⁺) deintercalate from the graphite anode, travel through the liquid electrolyte (typically LiPF₆ dissolved in an organic carbonate solvent), and intercalate into the layered metal oxide cathode. This ion movement drives electron flow through the external circuit, powering the motor. On charging, the process is reversed.

Section 05

Opportunities and Challenges

Environmental Opportunities

The most compelling benefit of renewable energy in transport is the dramatic reduction in greenhouse gas emissions and local air pollutants. A comprehensive analysis by CEEW estimates that if India achieves its 2030 EV targets, the transport sector could avoid approximately 846 million tonnes of cumulative CO₂ emissions between 2020 and 2035. At the city level, replacing diesel buses with electric alternatives can reduce PM2.5 concentrations along bus corridors by 20-30%, directly improving respiratory health outcomes for millions of urban residents. Additionally, electric motors produce near-zero noise pollution compared to ICE vehicles, addressing a significant but often overlooked urban environmental challenge [12].

Economic Opportunities

The EV value chain — spanning lithium-ion cell manufacturing, battery pack assembly, motor production, charging infrastructure deployment, and battery recycling — represents a massive new industrial sector. Industry body ACMA estimates that the Indian EV component market alone will reach $100 billion by 2030, with the potential to create 10 million direct and indirect jobs. Furthermore, by reducing oil imports, the transition could save India upwards of ₹1 lakh crore annually in foreign exchange outflows, strengthening the rupee and improving the current account balance [13].

Section 06

Government Policy Framework

The Government of India has deployed a multi-pronged policy framework to catalyze the transition to renewable energy in transportation. These policies span demand incentives, supply-side manufacturing support, infrastructure mandates, and long-term strategic commitments. The effectiveness of these policies will ultimately determine the pace and scale of the transition.

Launched 2019 • ₹10,000 Crore

FAME-II Scheme

Faster Adoption and Manufacturing of Electric Vehicles Phase II provides demand subsidies for EV buyers and supports over 7,400 electric buses. It also funds charging stations at every 25 km on highways.

Launched 2013

National Electric Mobility Mission Plan

Targets 6-7 million electric and hybrid vehicles on Indian roads annually by 2030, supported by a comprehensive roadmap for manufacturing, standards, and technology development.

Target 2025-26

Ethanol Blending Programme (EBP)

Achieved 12% blending in 2024, targeting E20 (20% ethanol in petrol) nationwide by 2025-26. Projected to save ₹30,000 crore annually in crude oil imports.

Launched 2023 • ₹19,744 Crore

National Green Hydrogen Mission

Aims to establish India as a global hub for green hydrogen with 5 MTPA production capacity by 2030, with transport being a key end-use application.

Launched 2022 • ₹18,100 Crore

PLI Scheme for ACC Battery

Production Linked Incentive for Advanced Chemistry Cell battery manufacturing — 50 GWh domestic capacity target to reduce import dependency on Chinese cells.

Target 2070

Net Zero 2070 Commitment

India's COP26 pledge to achieve net-zero carbon emissions by 2070. Transport decarbonization is a central pillar, requiring complete phase-out of fossil-fuel vehicles by mid-century.

Section 07

Future Outlook

The trajectory of India's transport sector over the next two to five decades will be defined by the interplay of technological maturity, policy consistency, infrastructure investment, and consumer behavior change. The following timeline outlines key milestones that will shape this transformation:

2025-26

E20 Ethanol Blending Achieved Nationwide

20% ethanol blend in petrol across all fuel stations. Estimated annual CO₂ reduction of 7% from the petrol vehicle fleet and crude oil import savings of ₹30,000 crore.

2027

500,000+ Public EV Charging Points

Rapid densification of charging infrastructure driven by FAME-II mandates and private investment from companies like Tata Power, Adani, and ChargeZone.

2028

Green Hydrogen Buses Deployed in 10+ Cities

Pilot programs scaled up in Delhi, Mumbai, Pune, Bengaluru, and Leh. Intercity hydrogen bus corridors established on select national highways.

2030

30% of New Vehicle Sales are Electric

India achieves its EV penetration target, led by two-wheelers (50%+ electric) and three-wheelers (70%+ electric). 500 GW renewable energy ensures clean charging.

2030

Indian Railways Achieves Net Zero

Complete broad-gauge electrification. Solar and wind installations along railway corridors. The world's fourth-largest railway becomes climate-neutral.

2040

Phase-Out of New ICE Vehicle Sales Begins

Regulatory frameworks mandate all new passenger vehicle sales to be zero-emission (electric or hydrogen), aligning with European and American timelines.

2070

Net Zero India

Transport sector fully decarbonized. A mix of EVs, hydrogen, and renewable-powered rail delivers zero-emission mobility across the world's most populous nation.

Conclusion

Summary and Recommendations

Renewable energy integration in India's transportation sector represents one of the most significant structural transformations in the nation's economic history. The shift from fossil-fuel dependence to an ecosystem powered by electric vehicles, green hydrogen, biofuels, and electrified railways offers enormous environmental, economic, and strategic benefits — from slashing CO₂ emissions and air pollution to strengthening energy security and creating millions of new jobs. However, the transition is neither automatic nor guaranteed. It requires sustained policy commitment, massive infrastructure investment, breakthroughs in battery chemistry and recycling, development of a skilled workforce, and — critically — the simultaneous greening of India's electricity grid. Without clean power generation, even universal EV adoption will merely shift emissions from the tailpipe to the power plant. The challenge is immense, but so is the opportunity: getting this transition right could position India as a global leader in sustainable mobility and green industrial manufacturing for decades to come.
